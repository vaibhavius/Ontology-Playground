1. What is an Ontology?

An ontology is a formal description of the concepts in a domain and the relationships between them. Think of it as a blueprint that tells a system: "these are the things that exist, these are their properties, and this is how they connect."

In Microsoft Fabric, ontologies power IQ — the intelligent query layer that lets users ask natural-language questions about their data. The ontology tells Fabric what entities exist (like Customer, Order, Product), what properties they have, and how they relate to each other.

Key idea: An ontology is not the data itself — it's the shape of the data. A "Customer" entity type doesn't contain any customer records; it describes what a customer is (has a name, an email, places orders).

Every ontology has three building blocks:

  • Entity types — the concepts (Customer, Product, Order)
  • Properties — the attributes of each entity (name, price, date)
  • Relationships — how entities connect (a Customer places an Order)

Let's explore these ideas through real examples.

2. A First Example: Cosmic Coffee

Imagine you're building a data model for a coffee chain. You need to track customers, the orders they place, the products in each order, the stores where orders are fulfilled, and the suppliers who provide the beans.

The ontology below captures all of this. Click on any node to see its properties — for example, click Customer to see it has a name (identifier), email, and loyaltyTier. Click on an edge like places to see the relationship between Customer and Order.

Notice the cardinalities: a Customer can place many Orders (one-to-many), each Order is processed at one Store (many-to-one), and Products are sourced from Suppliers (many-to-many). These cardinalities tell Fabric how to join tables when answering questions like "Which supplier provides the products in order #1234?"

Try it: Switch to the RDF Source tab to see the same ontology expressed as RDF/XML — the W3C standard format that Fabric uses under the hood.

3. E-Commerce: Products, Carts, and Orders

Online retail is a classic ontology domain. A buyer browses products, adds them to a shopping cart, then places an order. After receiving a product, they might write a review.

This ontology models that flow. The Shopping Cart entity is interesting — it sits between Buyer and Product, representing a transient "intent to purchase" before the Order is finalized.

Why model Reviews as a separate entity?

You might wonder: why not just add a "review" property on Product? Because a review belongs to a specific buyer and a specific product — it's a relationship, not just a property. By making Review its own entity, you can ask questions like "Show me all 5-star reviews by buyers who spent over $100" — something that requires joining Buyer → Review → Product.

4. Healthcare: Patients, Providers, and Prescriptions

Healthcare data is notoriously complex. This ontology simplifies it to five core concepts: Patients, Providers (doctors), Appointments, Diagnoses, and Prescriptions.

The key insight is the chain of events: a Patient has an Appointment with a Provider, who makes a Diagnosis, which leads to a Prescription. Click the edges to trace this path.

Identifier properties (marked with 🔑 in the inspector) are special — they uniquely identify each record. A Patient's identifier might be patientId, while a Prescription's identifier is prescriptionId. Fabric uses these to resolve entities when answering queries.

6. Manufacturing: Machines, Sensors, and Quality

In Industry 4.0, factories generate massive amounts of sensor data. This ontology models the essential concepts: Machines that are monitored by Sensors, Work Orders that Machines execute, Parts that Work Orders consume, and Quality Checks that inspect the finished Parts.

This is a great example of an ontology where the sensor → machine → work order → part → quality check chain enables end-to-end traceability. If a quality check fails, you can trace back through the ontology to find which machine, which sensor readings, and which work order were involved.

7. University: Students, Courses, and Departments

Our final example models a university. Students enroll in Courses through Enrollments (a many-to-many relationship modeled as its own entity). Professors teach Courses and belong to Departments.

Why is Enrollment a separate entity?

Just like Review in the E-Commerce ontology, Enrollment is a junction entity — it represents the many-to-many relationship between Students and Courses, but it also carries its own properties (like grade and semester). Without it, you couldn't answer "What grade did student X get in course Y in Fall 2025?"

8. Embed These on Your Own Site

Every visualization on this page is a self-contained embed widget. To add one to your own page, copy this snippet: 

<div class="ontology-embed"
     data-catalogue-id="official/cosmic-coffee"
     data-catalogue-base-url="YOUR_SITE_URL/"
     data-theme="dark"
     data-height="500px">
</div>
<script src="YOUR_SITE_URL/embed/ontology-embed.js"></script>

Replace YOUR_SITE_URL with the URL of your deployed Ontology Playground site. You can also:

  • Use data-ontology-url to load from any RDF/XML or JSON URL
  • Use data-ontology-inline with a base64-encoded JSON ontology
  • Set data-theme="light" for light backgrounds
  • Adjust height with data-height="400px"

You can also grab the embed code from the Gallery in the main app — click the share icon on any ontology card to copy a ready-to-paste snippet.
